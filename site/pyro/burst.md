# Burst charges

> Burst charges are the driving force behind the expansion of many pyrotechnic effects, providing the rapid release of gas and pressure needed to open shells and disperse visual elements. Designed to burn quickly and predictably, they enable controlled spread rather than destructive force, making them a critical yet carefully regulated component in professional pyrotechnics.

Burst charges are a core functional element in many pyrotechnic effects, but they’re also one of the most safety-critical parts. This description is high-level and descriptive, not instructional.

---

A burst charge is a small energetic component inside a pyrotechnic device whose job is to rapidly release gas and pressure. That sudden pressure does one or more of the following:

- Break open a shell or casing
- Scatter stars, sparks, or color-producing elements
- Initiate secondary visual or auditory effects

In short: it’s the “push” that makes the effect expand or separate.

---

When ignited, a burst charge:

- Burns or deflagrates very rapidly (not a high explosive in the detonation sense)
- Produces hot gas and pressure
- Uses that pressure to mechanically disperse other pyrotechnic components

The effect is expansion, not shattering—controlled force rather than violent fragmentation.

---

Burst charges are designed to be:

- Fast-burning compared to lift or delay compositions
- Gas-producing, not just flame-producing
- Consistent and predictable, so effects open evenly

They are typically:

- Enclosed within a shell or compartment
- Separated from decorative compositions until activation
- Sized carefully relative to the payload they must disperse

---

Burst charges appear in many forms of pyrotechnics:

- **Aerial shells** – to break the shell and spread stars symmetrically
- **Stage effects** – to open flash pots or confetti effects
- **Special effects devices** – to trigger rapid transitions or reveals

The same conceptual role applies across all of these: **controlled dispersion**.

---

Because burst charges involve energetic materials and pressure, they are:

- Highly regulated in most countries
- Restricted to licensed manufacturers and operators
- A major focus of safety standards and testing

Improper design or handling can cause:

- Premature ignition
- Over-pressurization
- Unintended fragmentation

That’s why professional pyrotechnics treats burst charges as engineering components, not just “extra powder.”
