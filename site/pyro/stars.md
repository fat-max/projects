Stars are **discrete pyrotechnic units** designed to produce sustained visual effects such as color, light, sparks, or motion when ignited. They function as the primary **effect-producing elements** in many fireworks and special-effects devices, translating chemical energy into controlled optical output over a defined burn time.

Once ignited, a star burns in a **self-sustaining and predictable manner**, emitting light and/or particles according to its formulation and physical geometry. Unlike burst charges or primes, stars are not intended to generate pressure or initiate other components; their role is purely expressive rather than mechanical.

Stars are typically engineered to balance:

- **Color purity or spark character**
- **Burn duration and intensity**
- **Structural integrity** during handling, launch, and dispersion
- **Consistent ignition and performance** across multiple units

From a systems perspective, stars are often used in combination with:

- **Primes**, to ensure reliable ignition
- **Burst charges**, to disperse them spatially
- **Shell or device geometry**, which influences pattern and visual symmetry

Their shape, size, and density affect how they burn and how they move through space, contributing to effects such as trailing, glittering, pulsation, or steady illumination.

Because stars contain energetic materials and are central to the visual outcome of a device, their manufacture and use are **subject to regulation and quality control**, particularly with regard to uniformity, stability, and safety.
